WCAG 2.3.1 — Three Flashes or Below Threshold

Web content must not flash more than 3 times per second in any one-second period, unless the flash is below the General Flash Threshold — which accounts for both the luminance change and the area of the flashing content on screen. Flash rates between 3 Hz and 50 Hz are the most likely to trigger photosensitive seizures.

What counts as a flash
A pair of opposing luminance changes — one transition to bright and one to dark. Three flashes per second means six state changes every second.
Blink vs. flash
Below ~3 Hz the effect is perceived as blinking — distracting but not a seizure risk. Above 3 Hz it becomes flashing, which is the threshold WCAG 2.3.1 addresses.
General Flash Threshold
A flash above 3 Hz may still comply if its screen area is small enough (less than 25% of the 10° visual field, roughly 341 × 256 px at a typical viewing distance) and the luminance change between states is below a defined level.
Duration and the one-second window
The rule applies to any one-second period. A very brief animation — short enough that no one-second window contains 3 complete flashes — falls outside the rule's scope, though this does not make high-frequency flashes inherently safe.
Testing tools
PEAT (Photosensitive Epilepsy Analysis Tool) and the Harding FPA test analyse video content against the full General Flash Threshold, including area and colour calculations.
